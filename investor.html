Why DDT is defensible

The structural moat.

01
First filed, first standard

DDT's proposed org.c2pa.digital-likeness assertion — filed at CAWG and DIF as Issue #259 — is the only active implementation addressing subject-level consent in this namespace. No competing implementation exists. The filing is timestamped. The standard that gets ratified first becomes the one everyone else accommodates.

02
The subject layer no one else built

A studio that registers a performer scan in an asset-level consent registry registers consent under the studio's identity, not the performer's. DDT registers consent under the performer's decentralized identifier. No existing system does this. Not DECORAIT. Not C2PA. Not any existing rights expression framework. This distinction is the foundational architectural gap DDT fills.

03
Live revocability is structurally different

As OCSP answers "is this TLS certificate currently valid?" the IPS responder answers "is this identity use currently authorized?" That question has never had a live, revocable answer. Static consent documents cannot produce one. Every platform that ingests, generates, or distributes identity-derived content now needs this answer. DDT provides it.

04
The Verification Receipt closes the loop

Every IPS query generates a cryptographically signed, time-stamped Verification Receipt — proof that the consent check happened. That receipt is the audit document for platforms, unions, and legal proceedings. No static consent system can produce one. It is the document that makes DDT legally defensible at scale.

05
Union distribution creates instant density

SAG-AFTRA, WGA, Equity UK: each union that distributes CRO infrastructure to members creates instant network density. Every performer who activates a CRO is a node in the network. Platforms cannot ignore a consent layer that covers the majority of professional talent.

06
Neutral infrastructure, not DRM

DDT signals. Downstream systems act. DDT does not block, enforce, moderate, or host content. This distinction preempts the platform liability objection, the DRM objection, and the content moderation objection simultaneously — and makes DDT adoptable by every party in the ecosystem.
